# Step 3 — Build the Front Ladder

You're going to take **2 legs** and **2 of the prepped 9" rails** and join them into a flat "ladder": two vertical legs with a horizontal rail at the top and another partway down. This is the front face of the stand. Step 4 will be the same thing again for the back. Step 5 will tie the two ladders together.

Doing the front and back as separate flat sub-assemblies — instead of trying to build the whole 3D frame at once — is what makes this manageable. Each ladder lays flat on the floor while the glue sets, gravity holds the joints together, and clamping is easy.

## What to grab

- [ ] **2 of the 4 legs** (32.25"). Either two will do; just set the other two aside for Step 4.
- [ ] **2 of the 4 prepped 9" rails** (the ones with pocket holes from Step 2). Same — pick any two.
- [ ] **Wood glue** (Titebond II or III)
- [ ] **2.5" coarse-thread pocket hole screws** — you'll drive 4 of these
- [ ] **Drill/driver** with the **square (Robertson) bit** that came with the pocket screws — pocket screws use a square drive, not Phillips
- [ ] **Tape measure + pencil**
- [ ] **At least 2 clamps** (bar clamps, F-clamps, or quick clamps — anything that opens to 16"+)
- [ ] **A speed square or framing square** (or any reliable right-angle reference)
- [ ] **Damp rag** for squeeze-out

## Layout (do this dry first, no glue)

Work on the floor or a flat workbench. **Lay the two legs flat on their wide (3½") faces, parallel, with their tops aligned.** Both pocket-hole rails go *between* the legs.

Orient everything so:

- The **legs are 16" apart, outside-to-outside.** With each leg 3½" wide and a 9" rail between them, that math works out: 3.5 + 9 + 3.5 = 16. The rail length sets the spacing automatically — you don't need to measure it.
- The rails sit **on edge** between the legs (their 3½" faces vertical, their 1½" faces touching the leg). Pocket holes face **up** (toward the ceiling). You'll drive screws down into the holes from above.
- **Top rail:** flush with the top ends of both legs.
- **Bottom rail:** measure **10" up from the bottom end** of each leg and mark a line. The bottom edge of the rail sits on that line. (This 10" gap is where the shelf will eventually live — the rail is what the shelf will sit on top of.)

Slide everything around until the joints close up tight (no daylight between rail end and leg face). If a joint won't close, the cut wasn't quite square — sand the offending end lightly and try again.

## Glue and clamp the top joint first

Always do **one rail at a time** — glue, clamp, screw, *then* the next rail. Trying to glue both rails at once is a recipe for misalignment.

1. **Slide the top rail out** of position by a few inches so you can reach the joint.
2. **Apply a thin, even bead of glue** to the end grain of the rail (the 1½" × 3½" rectangle at the rail end). End grain soaks up glue, so this side gets the glue. A small puddle, not a thick stripe.
3. **Slide the rail back into position** — flush with the top of the leg, tight against the leg's inside face.
4. **Wipe squeeze-out** with the damp rag. Glue dries clear-ish but it'll show under the spray paint.
5. **Clamp.** Place a clamp across the joint, pulling the leg snug against the rail end. One clamp per joint is enough; you'll have one on each end of the rail. The clamp is doing the *holding*; the screw will do the *locking*.
6. **Check square.** Hold a speed square in the inside corner where the rail meets the leg — the leg should be perpendicular to the rail. If it's slightly off, nudge it before driving the screw.

## Drive the pocket screws

With the joint clamped:

1. Chuck the **square driver bit** into your drill (it ships with the pocket screws — looks like a Robertson bit, ~6" long for reach).
2. **Drop a 2.5" pocket screw into the pocket hole** — it'll seat itself in the counterbore.
3. **Drive at low-to-medium speed.** The screw self-feeds along the angled tunnel and bites into the leg. Stop driving the moment the head bottoms out in the pocket — stripping the wood here is easy if you keep going.
4. **Repeat for the other end of the same rail.** One screw per pocket hole, two pockets per rail = two screws for the top rail.

The clamp can come off as soon as both screws are driven on that rail — the screws now hold the joint while the glue cures.

## Now the bottom rail

Same procedure, exactly:

1. Find the **10" mark** on each leg (you should already have it pencilled).
2. Glue the rail's end grain on both ends.
3. Slide it into place — bottom edge on the 10" line, pocket holes facing up.
4. Clamp at each joint.
5. Square-check, then drive a 2.5" pocket screw into each pocket hole.

When this rail is done, you've used 4 screws total on this ladder.

## Sanity checks

- [ ] **4 pocket screws driven**, all the way down, none stripped
- [ ] **Top rail flush** with the leg tops (no rail sticking up past the legs, no leg sticking up past the rail)
- [ ] **Bottom rail's bottom edge** sitting on the 10" line on each leg — both ends at exactly 10", not 9¾" on one side and 10¼" on the other
- [ ] **Legs are parallel.** Measure between the two legs at the very top and at the very bottom — both measurements should be the same. If they're off by more than ~⅛", the ladder will be a parallelogram instead of a rectangle. Loosen one screw, nudge, retighten.
- [ ] **Front face is flat.** Run your hand across the front face of the assembled ladder — leg fronts and rail fronts should all be in the same plane. If a rail is set back ⅛" from the leg fronts that's fine (and actually invisible once painted), but it shouldn't be proud (sticking out past) the legs.
- [ ] **Glue squeeze-out wiped** off all four joints

## Let it sit

Glue is what's actually doing most of the structural work. The screws just hold things tight while it cures.

**Leave the ladder undisturbed for at least 30 minutes** before moving it, ideally an hour. Titebond reaches working strength in about 30 min and full strength in 24 hours. You don't need to wait 24 hours to start Step 4 — you just shouldn't be picking up or flexing this ladder in the next half hour.

## Why we're not adding the side rails yet

The 13" side rails (the longer ones) connect the front ladder to the back ladder. There is no back ladder yet, so there's nothing to connect to. We build the back ladder identically in Step 4, then in Step 5 we'll stand both ladders up, lay the 13" rails between them, and screw through the leg faces.

This is also why we're using **pocket screws on the 9" rails specifically**: the legs sit *outside* the 9s, so a normal screw driven from the outside of the leg would have its head visible on the front of the finished stand. Pocket screws come from the inside, hidden. The 13" rails will use through-screws because the legs sit *inside* of them (on the side faces, not the front), and a screw head on the side face is fine.

## When you're done

One flat, square front ladder lying on the floor. Two legs joined by two 9" rails. **Say "ready" and I'll give you Step 4** — building the back ladder, which is literally the same step again with the other 2 legs and the other 2 prepped 9" rails.
