# Step 2 — Drill Pocket Holes in the 9" Rails

Grab the four **9" rails** (the shortest 2×4 pieces, from their own pile in Step 1). Each one gets **one pocket hole at each end** — 8 pocket holes total. That's the entire step. We're not assembling anything yet; we're just prepping joinery while the pieces are still loose and easy to clamp.

## What a pocket hole is (in 30 seconds)

A pocket hole is an angled screw hole that enters one face of a board and exits the end grain. When you later drive a screw through it into a second piece, the angle pulls the two pieces tight together — strong joint, no visible screw head on the outside of the stand.

The **Kreg jig** is a little metal block that holds the drill bit at exactly the right angle (15°) and sets the depth. Clamp it to the board, drop the stepped bit through the guide, drill until it stops. That's the whole tool.

## What to grab

- [ ] All four 9" rails
- [ ] Kreg pocket hole jig (K4, Mini, or whatever you/your brother-in-law has — any model that takes 1½" stock is fine)
- [ ] The stepped drill bit that came with the jig
- [ ] Stop collar + hex key (ships with the jig)
- [ ] Drill/driver
- [ ] A solid surface to clamp against — workbench, picnic table, sawhorse
- [ ] Pencil, for marking the inside face
- [ ] Shop vac or broom — pocket hole drilling kicks out a surprising pile of chips

## Jig setup (one-time, ~2 minutes)

You set this once and then drill all 8 holes without touching it again.

1. **Material thickness:** slide the jig's adjustable block to the **1½"** setting. Every Kreg jig has this line marked on the housing. 2×4 actual dimension is 1½" on the narrow face, which is what the jig will clamp to.
2. **Bit depth collar:** slide the stop collar onto the stepped bit until it lines up with the **1½" mark** on the depth gauge printed on top of the jig (or on the bit itself). Snug the set screw with the hex key. This is what stops the bit from punching all the way through the end grain.
3. Chuck the bit into your drill and tighten it **hard** — this bit is long and will walk if the chuck is loose.

## Drill one pocket hole (repeat 8 times)

For **each end of each 9" rail**:

1. **Pick the inside face.** The rails have two wide (3½") faces. Pick one to be the "inside" face — the one the pocket hole will live on. It doesn't matter which of the two you pick, but **use the same face on all four rails** so they orient consistently when you assemble. Mark it with a pencil checkmark if you'll forget.
2. **Clamp the jig** to the rail so its drill guide sits flush with the **end** of the board and roughly centered across the 3½" width. Most jigs have a built-in clamp lever — tighten it until the rail won't move.
3. **Drill.** Run the drill at medium speed, push the bit through the guide, and keep steady pressure until the stop collar bottoms out on the jig (you'll feel it stop, hard). Back the bit out while it's **still spinning** — if you stop first, the bit can bind in the hole.
4. **Flip, repeat.** Un-clamp, spin the rail end-for-end, and drill the other end the same way. Keep the same face up each time.
5. Move to the next rail.

Takes maybe 1-2 minutes per hole once you have a rhythm.

## Sanity checks

- [ ] **8 pocket holes total** — 4 rails × 2 ends
- [ ] Every hole on a given rail is on the **same wide face**, and **all four rails** have their holes on the same face (orientation matches)
- [ ] Each hole **broke through cleanly** at the end grain — look at the end of each rail; you should see a little oval opening where the bit came through. If any hole didn't quite punch through, bump the stop collar slightly deeper and redrill just that one.
- [ ] **No holes on the 13" rails.** The 13s don't get pocket holes — they get through-screws from the leg face in a later step. If you catch yourself drilling one, stop.
- [ ] **No holes in the legs or plywood.** Same reason.
- [ ] Chips cleared out of each hole (tap the rail on the bench, or puff them out)

## Why only the 9" rails get this treatment

The 9" front/back rails sit *between* the legs, so the leg is on the **outside** of the joint. You can't drive a screw from the leg face into the rail end without the screw head showing on the outside of the stand. Pocket screws solve this: the screw comes from **inside the rail**, through the angled pocket hole, and bites into the leg from the hidden side. Clean exterior, strong joint.

The 13" left/right rails don't have this problem — they sit *outside* the front and back assemblies, so you can just drive a normal screw from the leg's outer face into the rail end. Simpler and stronger for those pieces. Right joinery for each rail.

## When you're done

Four rails, eight clean pocket holes, all on matching faces. Put the prepped 9" rails back in their pile (holes up, so you don't lose track of which face is which). The 13" rails, legs, and plywood squares are untouched — leave them alone.
